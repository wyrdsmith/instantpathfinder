# Instant Pathfinder: Agentic Architecture Planning

This document serves as a workspace for brainstorming and planning the multi-agent system that will generate Pathfinder 2e adventure modules. 

## 1. Architecture Paradigm: GraphFlow

The app will make use of GraphFlow in the AgentChat API from Microsoft's AutoGen, which will allow for a stateful, multi-agent conversation. Custom 
agents will be created for each stage and process of the adventure generation pipeline using ChromaDB to store conversation history and metadata. The 
agents will have access to tools to facilitate their work as needed such as querying an SQLite database of Pathfinder 2e rules and data. The primary model will be an Ollama local LLM.

## 2. The Process of Generating an Adventure Module

### Definitions and Guidelines

1. An adventure module is defined as a quest with a number of encounters for which the experience point total is 1000 (the experience required to gain a single level) and the total treasure is equal to the recommended treasure by level for a party of X characters of Y level.
2. There is a GM and X player characters. The default value for X is 4. The generator will handle adjustments in balance for different values of X.
    1. The GM guides the players through the quest through descriptions of the environment and NPCs provided by the generator based on the quest, adjudicating the results of the player characters' actions, and describing the results of each encounter and each scene.
    2. The player characters are level Y. The default value for Y is 1. The generator will handle adjustments in balance for different values of Y.
3. A quest is defined as a story that follows the three act structure. The three acts are defined as follows:
    1. Act I: The Setup. This act introduces the characters, the setting, and the conflict.
    2. Act II: The Confrontation. This act builds on the conflict introduced in Act I and escalates the stakes.
    3. Act III: The Resolution. This act resolves the conflict introduced in Act II and provides a sense of closure.
4. Each act has a number of scenes. A scene is defined as an encounter that takes place within a single location.
    1. The number of scenes is the minimum number of encounters required to advance the plot in a meaningful way for that act.
    2. A scene establishes the location based on the needs of the quest and the preceding encounter, introduces the encounter, and provides a resolution for that encounter.
    3. A scene is resolved once the encounter is resolved. The scene resolution follows the encounter resolution and describes the immediate consequences of the encounter. The resolution of the scene may also include a description of the rewards earned by the players within the scene as well as the possibility of resting or downtime before the next scene.
    4. Each scene builds upon the previous scene just as each act builds upon the previous act.
    5. Not every scene is in a new location, some scenes may take place in the same location as a preceding scene, such as in a dungeon where multiple events might happen, or an already visited location.
    6. Not every scene immediately follows the previous scene. There may be a significant amount of time between scenes, or the characters may travel to a different location between scenes. These transitions should be handled narratively.
5. The locations of scenes are collectively known as a region. A region is a geographic area that contains settlements such as villages, towns, and cities, as well as natural locations such as forests, mountains, rivers, and dungeons.
    1. The region is defined by the milieu, themes, and setting of the quest. For example, an urban fantasy quest might take place in a region that consists of a single large city and includes a number of districts, underground tunnel networks, and abandoned buildings. A high fantasy quest might take place in a region that includes a number of villages, towns, cities, forests, mountains, rivers, and dungeons.
    2. A region should contain a number of settlements and natural locations roughly proportional to its size and the scope of the quest. So a quest taking place in a small rural area might contain only a single village and a few surrounding natural locations, while a quest taking place in a large city might contain a number of different districts and a small number of surrounding natural locations (if any).
    3. A region might not include any settlements or natural locations depending on the quest. For instance, a quest that takes place entirely within a city might not include any natural locations, while a quest that takes place entirely within a dungeon might not include any settlements or natural locations.
6. A scene's location must make sense in the context of the region, the preceding scene, the encounter within the scene, the anticipated next scene, and the overall quest structure.
    1. A location can be of any size, from a single room in a dungeon or building, to an entire building such as an inn or shop, to a district within a town or city, to an entire town or city, to a wilderness area.
    2. The size of a location should be appropriate for the encounter taking place. For example, a social encounter might take place in a single room, while a combat encounter might take place in a large room (or multiple connected rooms/areas). A skill challenge might take place in a location that is either large or small depending on the nature of the challenge. For example, a skill challenge to disarm a trap might take place in a room, while a skill challenge to evade capture might take place in a city district or wilderness area.
    3. The location of a scene should make sense in the context of the region. For example, a quest taking place in a small rural area should not have a scene that takes place in a large city. A quest taking place in a city should have a location that is appropriate for the scene.
7. An encounter is defined as a combat encounter, a social encounter, a skill challenge, or a hazard.
    1. When an encounter is created its type is chosen based on the needs of the scene and its place in the story. So the first scene of Act I will almost always be a social encounter, while the last scene of Act III will almost always be a combat encounter.
        1. Combat encounters involve enemy creatures appropriately chosen from a database table of Pathfinder 2e creatures. Combat encounters are resolved once all enemies are dead, incapacitated, or have fled.
            1. Combat encounters have an experience budget represented by the encounter's threat level. The budget is determined by choosing the appropriate row from the threats table based on the encounter's threat level. Adding a creature costs experience based on its level relative to the party's level. The sum of the experience cost of all enemy creatures in the encounter must be equal to or less than the budget. The minimum number of enemies in a combat encounter is 1. The maximum number of enemies in a combat encounter is 20.
            2. To determine how much experience a creature costs, subtract the party's level from the creature's level then look up the difference in level on the creature experience table in the database, the corresponding experience value is the experience cost of the creature. For example, if the level difference is 0, the experience cost is 40.
            3. A creature's appropriateness is determined by the level of the creature relative to the party's level, the threat level of the encounter, the location of the encounter, the scene's place in the story, and the overall theme and tone of the quest. Creatures more than 4 levels lower than the party's level are never considered appropriate, nor are creatures 4 levels higher than the party's level.
            4. When determining which creatures to add to an encounter, beyond considering the experience cost of the creature compared to the encounter's experience budget, is to consider the role a creature might play in the encounter relative to the encounter's threat level. Creature roles can be referenced in the creature experience table by the difference in level between the creature and party. For example, the role for a creature with a level 2 levels higher than the party level has a role of "Moderate- or severe-threat boss". This means that the creature could be a boss in a moderate- or severe-threat encounter.
        2. Social encounters involve multiple NPCs created for the needs of the quest and utilize the Influence subsystem of Pathfinder 2e. Each NPC in the social encounter is given additional attributes based on their personality and the needs of the quest to make them more challenging to influence. One of those attributes is a series of Influence thresholds which represent the number of successes needed in influencing the NPC to obtain a boon or achieve an objective from them. Players have a limited number of turns to influence NPCs based on the number of NPCs in the encounter.
            1. The attributes given to the NPCs in a social encounter in addition to their existing attributes are: Discovery, Influence, Influence Thresholds, Resistances, Weaknesses, and a Penalty.
                1. The Discovery attribute is a list of skills that the players can use to make checks and discover which skills can be used to influence the NPC or learn about the NPC's Resistances and Weaknesses. The difficulty for each type of skill check is determined by the encounter's level, with more appropriate skills having lower difficulties and less appropriate skills having higher difficulties. Perception can always be used in a discovery check eventhough it is not a skill. The difficulty for Perception checks is always based on the encounter's level. The appropriateness of skills is based on the NPC's personality, ancestry, background, and class or profession.
                2. The Influence attribute is a list of skills that can be used to influence the NPC, as well as the skill check modifier for each skill. The difficulty for each skill check is determined by the encounter's level, with more appropriate skills having lower difficulties and less appropriate skills having higher difficulties. The appropriateness of skills is based on the NPC's personality, ancestry, background, and class or profession.
                3. The Influence Thresholds attribute is a list of three thresholds, each representing a number of successes needed in influencing the NPC to achieve a certain objective. The thresholds are ordered from least to greatest, with the first threshold being the easiest to achieve and the last threshold being the hardest to achieve. The first threshold is the number of successes needed, 4, to obtain a minor boon. The second threshold is the number of successes, 6, needed to obtain a moderate boon. The third threshold is the number of successes, 8, needed to obtain a major boon. The boon awarded by the NPC should facilitate the party's progress in their quest, either by making future encounters easier, providing the party with information needed to progress, by providing the party with a resource that can be used in the future, or by representing a goal or objective that needs to be achieved.
                4. The Resistances attribute is a description of topics, subjects, or approaches that the players can use when speaking with the NPC, but are detrimental to the players' ability to influence the NPC. If a resistance topic or subject is broached in conversation or the players use a resistance approach, the players get a -2 to their rolls to influence the NPC.
                5. The Weaknesses attribute is the opposite of the Resistances attribute and is a description of topics, subject, or approaches that the players can use when speaking with the NPC that will grant them a bonus to their rolls to influence the NPC. If a weakness topic or subject is broached in conversation or the players use a weakness approach, the players get a +2 to their rolls to influence the NPC.
                6. The Penalty attribute is a taboo or inappropriate topic or subject of conversation or approach that the players can use to influence the NPC that will immediately reduce the number of turns the players have left to influence the NPC by 1.
            2. The number of turns the players have to influence each NPC in a scene is 3. Once the players have spent all 3 turns attempting to influence the NPC, that NPC can no longer be influenced. Once players have attempted to influence all NPCs in the social encounter, the encounter ends and successes for each NPC is counted and boons are awarded based on how many successes the players achieved for each NPC.
            3. All players act each turn in an order of their choosing as they roleplay to influence the NPC. Once all players have acted, the turn ends and the next turn begins. Players may choose to either perform a Discover skill check to learn more about the NPC and the NPC's attributes or perform an Influence skill check to attempt to influence the NPC.
        3. Skill challenges involve a series of obstacles that can be overcome by making skill checks. Skill challenges use the Victory Point subsystem where the party needs to accumulate a certain number of victory points to overcome the challenge.
            1. The number of victory points needed is from 15 to 25, depending on the skill challenge's place in the story with Act I encounters tending toward lower victory point requirements, Act II encounters tending toward higher victory point requirements, and Act III encounters tending toward the highest victory point requirements. A skill challenge can be failed if the party fails to accumulate the required number of victory points before running out of attempts. A skill challenge is resolved once the party has accumulated the required number of victory points or has failed to do so.
            2. A skill challenge is composed of a series of obstacles. Each obstacle is designed to hinder or harm the party in a way that is appropriate to the nature of the skill challenge, the previous obstacle, the next obstacle, the scene and the scene's place in the quest. The number of obstacles is from 8 to 12 with fewer obstacles for easier skill challenges and more obstacles for more difficult obstacle challenges. An obstacle can be anything from a trap to fallen rocks impeding a road way to an NPC(s) denying the party's progress to a magical hazard that must be disabled or circumvented.
            3. Each obstacle is resolved in a series of turns, where each turn represents a single action by each player character. A skill challenge has a maximum number of turns it can take to complete, determined by the quest based on the skill challenge's place in the story with Act I encounters tending toward lower maximum turn counts, Act II encounters tending toward higher maximum turn counts, and Act III encounters tending toward the highest maximum turn counts.
            4. The number of turns allowed to overcome each obstacle is from 3 to 5. The specific number of turns allowed is determined by the quest based on the skill challenge's place in the story with Act I encounters tending toward lower maximum turn counts, Act II encounters tending toward higher maximum turn counts, and Act III encounters tending toward the highest maximum turn counts.
            5. The means by which the party overcomes an obstacle is determined by the nature of the obstacle. When the obstacle is determined, from 2 to 4 skills are selected from the list of skills in the database that are appropriate to overcoming the obstacle. For example, if the obstacle is a locked door, the skills selected might be Athletics (to break it down), Thievery (to pick the lock), or Diplomacy (to convince someone to open it for you). The obstacle then has a number of successes required to overcome it, typically from 2 to 4. Once players have achieved the requisite number of successes, they have overcome the obstacle.
                1. The players choose which skill they use and the difficulty for each skill check is determined by the quest based on the skill challenge's encounter level modified by the skill challenge encounter's threat level. Further, the more appropriate a skill check is to overcome an obstacle might lower the difficulty by 2, whereas a less appropriate skill check might raise the difficulty by 2. This means the difficulty for each skill check for an obstacle is not always the same. In the earlier example of a locked door, if the door was made of iron, this means that the difficulty for an Athletics check would be increased by 2 as an iron door is hard to break down, but the difficulty for a Thievery check might be reduced by 2 as it might have a simple lock.
            6. An obstacle has both a success resolution and a failure resolution. In a success resolution, the obstacle is overcome in some way appropriate to the obstacle faced. In a failure resolution, the party is diverted around the obstacle, resulting in either greater difficulty in overcoming the next obstacle or some other negative consequence.
            7. Over the course of the skill challenge, each success against an obstacle counts as earning one victory point. Critical successes are worth two victory points. Critical failures subtract one victory point. Once all obstacles have been faced by the party, the skill challenge is resolved based on the number of victory points earned.
            8. If the party fails to achieve the required number of victory points, they can still receive a partial reward based on the number of victory points earned. At 5 victory points, the party receives 25% of the gold value of the reward or a minor boon that can aid them in their quest. At 10 victory points, the party receives 50% of the gold value of the reward or a boon. At 15 points, if the required victory points for a success is greater than 16, the party receives 75% of the gold value of the reward or a major boon. On a success, the party usually receives the entire reward, and if they earn an excess of victory points, they can earn a greater boon in addition to the reward.
        4. Hazards are similar to skill challenges, but they are traps that must be disarmed or avoided. Hazards are predefined in the database, but which hazard to use is determined by the quest by selecting the most relevant hazard to the scene from the hazards table in the database and should have a level the same as or up to 4 levels higher than the party. A hazard is resolved once the players have successfully disarmed or avoided the hazard. The skill checks and difficulties necessary to overcome or bypass a hazard are not determined by the generator.
            1. All hazards have a Stealth DC that must be met before the hazard is discovered. This means player characters are unaware of hazards in a scene until they either trigger the hazard or succeed on a Perception check to discover it. When a scene introduces the location, the hazard should not be revealed in the description of the location, but there might be clues to its existence hidden in the details.
            2. Because hazards are always hidden, the scene in which the hazard is encountered could begin in the same way a scene containing a social encounter or a skill challenge, but when the hazard is triggered, it becomes a threat and the players will have to immediately deal with it.
            3. Hazards can be employed in a combat encounter as if it was a creature. If a hazard is used in a combat encounter, it must fit within the experience budget of the encounter as outlined by combat encounters. When calculating the experience cost of a hazard used in combat, subtract the party's level from the hazard's level to determine the difference in level, then look up the experience cost for that difference in level to determine how much experience it is worth. The sum of all creatures and hazards in a combat encounter must be equal to or lower than the combat encounter's experience budget.
    2. An encounter's level begins as equal to the party's level. 
    3. Encounters have five threat levels: Trivial, Low, Moderate, Severe and Extreme and are found in the name column of the threats table of the database. Threat levels modify the encounter level. The threat level is determined by the encounter's place in the story with Act I encounters tending toward lower threat levels (Trivial or Low), Act II encounters tending toward higher threat levels (Low, Moderate, or Severe), and Act III encounters tending toward the highest threat levels (Severe or Extreme).
    4. All encounters reward experience based on the final encounter level after threat level modification. Specific encounter types have additional rewards:
        1. Combat encounters usually award treasure based on the final encounter level after threat level modification calculated from a table in the database that outlines the treasure value in gold by level. This value is then used to generate appropriate treasure for the encounter, including currency and items. Treasure awarded by combat encounters is usually found on the remains of the defeated enemies, but it can also be placed nearby and easily discovered in the location the scene takes place.
        2. Social encounters may reward boons such as currying favor with the NPCs influenced in the encounter as well as provide significant information for the party to use in future scenes. Boons may also include items appropriate for the party's level, especially if those items are useful for overcoming future challenges. 
        3. Skill challenges may award treasure based on the final encounter level after threat level modification calculated from a table in the database that outlines the treasure value in gold by level. This value is then used to generate appropriate treasure for the encounter, including currency and items. The manner in which the treasure is awarded should relate to the challenge the party faced and be placed within the location the scene took place. For example, if the party overcomes a number of traps, the treasure may be found behind a hidden wall that is revealed when the final trap is disarmed. Skill challenges may also reward boons that provide benefits for future scenes, such as obtaining the key to a previously locked door.
        4. Hazards may award treasure based on the final encounter level after threat level modification calculated from a table in the database that outlines the treasure value in gold by level. This value is then used to generate appropriate treasure for the encounter, including currency and items. The manner in which the treasure is awarded should relate to the nature of the hazard and be placed within the location the scene took place. For example, when the hazard is disabled, the treasure may be found within the mechanism that created the hazard or on the person of the creature that created the hazard. Hazards may also reward boons that provide benefits for future scenes, such as providing access to a previously inaccessible area or a shortcut through a previously impassable area.
        5. Encounters can also award key items necessary for the quest, act or scene. Key items are not included in the calculation of the total gold value of the reward.
    5. Encounters can involve a number of different checks such as perception checks (what player characters are aware of), skill checks (what player characters do) and save checks (how well player characters respond to sudden events). There are two types of Difficulties for checks: level and simple.
        1. Level DCs are determined by a table in the database based on the party's level.
            1. An example of a Level DC is in a skill challenge, wherein difficulties are set by the party's level. The party's level determines the encounter's level and is then modified by the threat level. The modified encounter level is then used to determine the difficulty of the skill checks required to overcome each obstacle in a challenge.
        2. Simple DCs are determined contextually based on the type of check and the expected level of training required to accomplish it. 
            1. An example of a Simple DC is a locked door. But in this case, the locked door would not have a difficulty set by the party's level, but rather contextually based on the type of check (unlocking a door) and the expected level of training from the Trainings table in the database. A mundane locked door would have a DC of 15 (Trained). If, however, the story described the door as being made of adamantine and being locked with a complex arcane mechanism, the difficulty would instead be 30 (Master).
8. Non-player characters or NPCs used in scenes are collectively known as the cast.
    1. Some NPCs will appear in multiple scenes throughout the quest, such as in a town where the PCs may need to interact with the same NPCs in different scenes. The generator should keep track of the NPCs it has created and the scenes they appear in, so that they can be consistently represented throughout the quest.
    2. Some NPCs may only appear for a single scene.
    3. When creating an NPC, the generator will use the following attributes: Name, Ancestry, Class or Profession, Level, Skills, Saves, Perception, Peronality and Appearance. These attributes will be used to determine the NPC's behavior and abilities.
        1. Name: The name is randomly generated using a first names table and a surnames table from the database. No two NPCs should have the same name, but related NPCs may have the same surname. If an NPC is related to another NPC, the generator should use the same surname for both NPCs. If an NPC is not related to any other NPC, the generator should use a random surname.
        2. Ancestry: The ancestry is determined randomly based on the rarity of ancestries in the ancestry table of the database.
        3. Class or Profession: An NPC has either a class or a profession, never both. If the NPC is central to the quest or otherwise integral to the story, the NPC is given a random class based on rarity from the classes table in the databse. If the NPC is not integral to the story, the NPC is given a random profession based on rarity from the professions table in the database. If an NPC is the antagonist of the story, the NPC is always integral to the story and is given an appropriate class based on the quest and the antagonist's role in the story.
        4. Level: The level of the NPC is equal to the party's level.
        5. Skills: An NPC has a number of skills, from 4 to 8, which are determined based on the NPC's class or profession and their role in the story. The skills are determined by referencing the skills table in the database and selected based on how appropriately the skills are suited to the NPC's class or profession and role in the story. Each skill has a modifier determined by referencing skill column in the creature stats table in the database based on the NPC's level.
        6. Saves: An NPC has three saves, Fortitude, Reflex and Will. When creating an NPC, determine which save is High, Medium or Low based on the NPC's class or profession and ancestry. For example, a wizard is likely to have a high Will save, while a fighter is likely to have a high Fortitude save.
            1. Fortitude represents a creature's physical endurance and resistance.
            2. Reflex represents a creature's reaction time and agility.
            3. Will represents a creature's mental fortitude and resilience.
        7. Perception: An NPC has a perception modifier referenced from the creature stats table in the database based on the NPC's level.
        8. Personality: This is a description of the NPC's demeanor and behavior. It should be consistent with the NPC's ancestry, class or profession, and role in the story.
        9. Appearance: This is a description of the NPC's appearance. It should be consistent with the NPC's ancestry, class or profession, and role in the story.
        10. Background: This is a description of the NPC's background. It should be consistent with the NPC's ancestry, class or profession, story setting, and role in the story.
    4. The size of the cast should be the minimum number of characters required to serve the needs of the quest, but never less than one. The size of the cast should be appropriate for the quest. So a quest taking place in a small rural area might have a cast of only a few NPCs, while a quest taking place in a large city might have a cast of a dozen or more NPCs.
    5. The cast should be diverse and interesting, with a variety of ancestries, classes and professions. The cast should also be appropriate for the quest. So a quest taking place in a small rural area should have a cast of characters that are appropriate for that area. A quest taking place in a city should have a cast of characters that are appropriate for that city.
    6. The cast should be consistent throughout the quest. So if an NPC is created for a specific scene, they should be represented consistently throughout that scene. If an NPC appears in multiple scenes, they should be represented consistently throughout all of the scenes they appear in.
    7. Not every scene needs a cast member; some scenes may have no cast members at all. For example, a combat encounter might not have any cast members, while a social encounter might have several.
9. Resting and daily preparations are critical to a party, especially after encounters that are resource intensive. Typically, Social encounters and Skill Challenges do not consume resources and so a rest will not be required after these encounters. Combat encounters and Hazards, however, are resource intensive and so a rest will be required after these encounters. Resting is divided into two types: a long rest and a short rest.
    1. A short rest typically involves a brief period of downtime, usually around 10 minutes, during which the party can rest and recover. A short rest can be included in the resolution of a scene if the scene ends in a way that allows for a brief period of downtime. For example, a social encounter that ends with the party winning the favor of a local lord might include a short rest wherein the party is treated as guests and given the opportunity to rest and recover.
    2. A long rest typically involves a longer period of downtime, usually around 8 hours, during which the party can rest and recover. A long rest can be included in the resolution of a scene if the scene ends in a way that allows for a longer period of downtime before the next scene. However, a scene could take place which interrupts a long rest, such as if the party is resting in a town and is awakened by the sound of an alarm bell. In this case, the long rest would be interrupted and the party would need to deal with the situation before they could complete their long rest. Additionally, a quest may span multiple days, in which case a long rest would be required after each day's worth of adventuring.
    3. Daily preparations are made only at the end of a long rest, typically after a full night's sleep. If the next scene after a scene ended with a long rest takes place during the day, the party will have had the opportunity to make their daily preparations. If the next scene after a scene ended with a long rest takes place during the night, the party will not have had the opportunity to make their daily preparations and will need to make them before they can engage in the next scene. This can create tension, as the party may need to make their daily preparations quickly before they can engage in the next scene.
    4. An adventure module, because it is made up of multiple encounters, necessarily happens over the span of several days and sometimes weeks. A scene, then, could represent as little time as a few minutes to as much time as several hours. When planning each scene, the generator should keep in mind how much time has passed since the previous scene, in order to maintain a sense of continuity and pacing and give the player characters an opportunity to rest or pass downtime.
        1. Downtime is defined as a period of time between scenes in which the party can perform maintenance on their equipment, do some shopping, or other downtime activities. Downtime is not required, but it is often a good idea to include it in an adventure module, as it can help to break up the action and give the player characters a chance to rest and recover.

### Generate Quest Data

1. Establish party level and number of player characters based on input parameters or config settings.
2. Establish quest themes and setting based on input parameters, config settings, or LLM generation.
3. Generate the quest data python object to collect the output of the following steps in a structured format.
4. Generate a plot hook for the quest based on the themes and setting. The plot hook should be a brief description that clearly states the premise of the quest and what the player characters need to accomplish to complete the quest. Output should be stored as a plot hook property in the quest data object.
5. Review the plot hook and compare it to a database table of previously generated plot hooks to determine uniqueness. If it too closely resembles a previous plot hook repeat steps 4 and 5. The plot hook should be replaced with the new plot hook, and the comparison should be repeated. This will continue until a unique plot hook is generated.
5. Using the themes, setting, and plot hook, generate a summary of the quest, high-lighting the central conflict and describing the arc of each of the three acts in the story. Output should be stored as a summary property in the quest data object.
6. Expand the description of each act into a summary of the act, including its general theme, the key plot points that will take place within it, and the key NPCs, key items and locations introduced in the act as they relate to the overall story and plot points. Output should be stored as a list in an acts property of the quest data object. Each act in the list should be an object with a summary property where the summary is stored.
    1. Act One is the introduction to the quest. It should introduce the player characters to the world, the story, and the characters they will meet. It should also establish the conflict and the stakes of the quest. Act One should end with the player characters firmly embroiled in the conflict with no easy way to turn back.
    2. Act Two is the rising action of the quest. It should continue the story, building on the conflict and stakes established in Act One. It should also introduce new characters and challenges that the player characters will face. Act Two should see the player characters working toward their goal while also dealing with new complications and adversaries. Act Two should end with the player characters in a dire situation, facing a significant setback or tough choice that will determine the outcome of the quest.
    3. Act Three is the climax and resolution of the quest. It should bring the story to a head, with the player characters facing the main conflict and the ultimate consequences of their decisions. Act Three should see the player characters working to resolve the main conflict while dealing with the complications and adversaries introduced in Act Two. Act Three should end with the player characters achieving their goal or failing to achieve it, and the resolution of the story as a result of their success or failure.
7. For each act, determine the number of scenes that will be required to tell the story based on the summary of the act and the number of key plot points. Because each scene contains an encounter and encounters earn the party experience and the sum of all encounters should equal 1000 experience points, the maximum number of scenes across all three acts is 25 (if all encounters are trivial) and the minimum number of scenes across all three acts is 7 (if all encounters are extreme).
    1. Act One should typically have the fewest scenes, as it is the introduction to the quest.
    2. Acts Two and Three will typically have more scenes than Act One.
    3. Act Two will typically have the most scenes, as it is the rising action of the quest.
    4. To assist in determining the number of scenes in each act, it may be helpful break the act's summary down into a series of steps. Each step should represent a distinct moment in the story that will need to be depicted for the players, building upon each other to tell the story of the act. Then, determine how many scenes are needed to depict all of the steps. For instance, the summary of the first act might be broken down into the following steps:
        1. The player characters are introduced to the main conflict and the stakes of the quest.
        2. The player characters are introduced to the main antagonists of the quest.
        3. The player characters are introduced to the main location of the quest.
        4. The player characters are introduced to the main goal of the quest.
8. For each scene, generate a summary of the scene in such a way that when all the summaries are read together, it tells the story of the quest, based on the summary of the quest. Make sure each scene follows the central conflict and arc of the act it's in, hitting the key plot points as outlined in the act's summary as necessary. A scene should be a self-contained unit of the story, with a clear beginning, middle, and end.
